Factors Affecting Heart Rate

  • Temperature

    The change in air or body temperature can influence your heart rate. Extreme temperatures, either too cold or too hot, can cause a slight increase in heart rate, usually 5 to 10 beats per minute. High humidity can also contribute to an elevated heart rate.

  • Body Position

    Your heart rate typically remains stable with different body positions. However, a sudden change, such as standing up, can lead to a temporary increase in heart rate for the first 15 to 20 seconds.

  • Emotions

    Intense emotions and stressful situations can lead to an elevated heart rate.

  • Body Size

    Individuals with severe obesity may experience a slightly faster pulse, generally not exceeding 100 beats per minute.

  • Caffeine and Nicotine

    Caffeine-containing beverages and nicotine can raise your heart rate. The peak effect of caffeine on heart rate occurs within one hour and lasts for 4 to 6 hours.

  • Alcohol

    Consuming more than five alcoholic beverages for men or four for women within two hours can lead to an irregular and rapid heart rate.

  • Saturated Fat and Sodium

    Excessive sodium intake can prompt an increase in heart rate as the heart pumps more blood to dilute it. Saturated fat consumption can raise cholesterol levels, potentially affecting heart activities and increasing heart rate.

  • Medications

    Beta blockers may result in a slow heart rate, while an excess of thyroid hormone can cause the heart to beat harder and faster, potentially triggering abnormal rhythms.

  • Age

    As you age, your resting heart rate (RHR) may not change significantly, but your training heart rate (THR) may become slower.

  • Gender

    The average resting heart rate is higher in women than in men. However, women generally have a lower risk of cardiac diseases.

  • Exercise

    Regular exercise gradually lowers your resting heart rate and reduces the risk of death associated with a high resting heart rate.
