Low Blood Sugar

  • Low blood sugar, or hypoglycemia, may not always exhibit symptoms and might not require immediate treatment. However, if left untreated and blood sugar drops significantly, it can pose a risk. Explore the information below to gain insights into low blood sugar and its management.

  • Diagnosing Hypoglycemia

    Recognizing hypoglycemia can be challenging as symptoms may not manifest until blood sugar drops below 55 mg/dL (3 mmol/L). To detect low blood sugar, a home blood sugar meter is essential. If the reading is under 70 mg/dL (3.9 mmol/L), it indicates low blood sugar.

    People with diabetes may experience hypoglycemia due to factors such as excessive insulin, skipped meals, or delayed eating after medication. Non-diabetics can also face low blood sugar, with reactive hypoglycemia occurring a few hours after eating, and fasting hypoglycemia linked to certain medications or underlying diseases.

  • Treating Hypoglycemia

    When hypoglycemia occurs, it can be managed by consuming 15-20 grams of fast-acting carbohydrates, such as juice, hard candy, or sugar tablets. Check blood sugar levels after 15 minutes, and if it remains below 70 mg/dL, repeat sugar intake at 15-minute intervals. Once blood sugar normalizes, consume a snack or meal for sustained stability.

    In severe cases, where hypoglycemia persists, seeking medical attention is crucial. Ambulance services may administer sugar injections or medications such as Baqsimi, Dasiglucagon, or Gvoke.

    If non-diabetic individuals frequently experience hypoglycemia, consulting a healthcare professional is advisable to identify potential causes like medications or tumors.

  • First Aid for Hypoglycemia

    Severe hypoglycemia, an emergency characterized by very low blood sugar, may occur in diabetics who skip meals, consume alcohol, or take excessive insulin. Recognize signs such as weakness, seizures, trembling, hunger, cold sweat, rapid pulse, and passing out.

    If the person is conscious:

    • Help them sit down and provide reassurance.
    • Offer 15-20 grams of simple sugar (hard candy, raisins, honey, fruit juice, non-diet soda, or jellybeans).
    • Check blood sugar after 15 minutes and repeat sugar intake if needed until recovery or medical assistance arrives.

    If the person is unconscious:

    • Call for urgent medical assistance and place the person on their side.
    • Do not allow the person to eat or drink anything.

    People with diabetes may encounter low blood sugar weekly, necessitating knowledge of identification and proper treatment for their well-being.
