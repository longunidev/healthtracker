Hypertension: Types and Symptoms

  • Delve into the world of blood pressure and the intricate workings of the cardiovascular system. Imagine the human cardiovascular system as a dynamic water circulatory system, where blood, like water, flows continuously, transporting oxygen and nutrients to organs and blood vessels.

    The heart serves as a relentless pump, propelling blood throughout the body's intricate network of vessels. As blood courses through, it exerts force against the vessel walls â€“ this force is known as blood pressure. Elasticity loss in blood vessels can hinder smooth blood flow, compelling the body to increase the force, leading to hypertension.

  • Understanding Hypertension Types

    Primary hypertension, the most common type, sees blood pressure rise with age, requiring lifelong medication for control. On the other hand, secondary hypertension stems from various conditions or medications and can improve when the underlying cause is addressed.

  • Understanding Hypertension Symptoms

    While often referred to as the "silent killer," hypertension may reveal symptoms in severe cases. Recognize these signs and seek immediate medical attention:

    • Headache

      A pulsating headache on both sides of the head may signal a hypertensive emergency, potentially damaging blood vessels in the brain.

    • Dizziness

      Sudden dizziness or loss of balance could be a sign of severe high blood pressure, a major contributor to stroke.

    • Nausea

      Experiencing sudden nausea and loss of appetite may indicate a hypertensive crisis, often accompanied by dizziness.

    • Shortness of Breath

      High blood pressure's impact on heart and lung function may lead to noticeable shortness of breath, especially during exercise.

    • Chest Pain

      Hypertension poses a significant risk for heart attack and heart failure, potentially causing chest pain or angina.

    • Blurred Vision

      Dangerously high blood pressure can damage small vessels in the eyes, causing blurred vision or even loss of vision.

    • Nosebleed

      While uncommon, extremely high blood pressure may lead to nosebleeds, signifying vessel damage and warranting immediate medical attention.
