Understanding Hypotension

  • Hypotension, characterized by consistently low blood pressure (lower than 90/60mmHg) or a sudden drop over 20 mmHg, may not require treatment if asymptomatic. However, accurate diagnosis and appropriate therapies are essential in the presence of symptoms or sudden falls in blood pressure. Explore the various types and causes of hypotension below.

  • Symptoms of Hypotension

    • Blurred vision
    • Dizziness or fainting
    • Inability to concentrate
    • Nausea
    • Rapid breathing
    • Fatigue

  • Types of Hypotension

    If you are experiencing these symptoms, consider checking your blood pressure and exploring the following types of hypotension:

    • Orthostatic Hypotension

      Occurs when standing up from a lying or sitting position, common in individuals over 65 and associated with certain medications.

    • Postprandial Hypotension

      Primarily seen in older adults after meals, resulting from inadequate heart rate increase and vasoconstriction.

    • Neurally Mediated Hypotension

      Linked to communication issues between the heart and brain, often triggered by prolonged standing or intense emotions.

  • Other Causes of Hypotension

    Consistent low blood pressure can also be attributed to various factors such as:

    • Pregnancy, hormonal changes
    • Arrhythmia (abnormal heartbeat)
    • Heart disease
    • Certain drug interactions
    • Endocrine disorders (diabetes, adrenal insufficiency, thyroid disease)

  • Reasons for Sudden Blood Pressure Fall

    If you experience a sudden fall in blood pressure, it could be due to:

    • Loss of blood from bleeding
    • Low or high body temperature
    • Heart muscle disease leading to heart failure
    • Sepsis (severe blood infection)
    • Severe dehydration from vomiting, diarrhea, or fever
    • Reaction to medication or alcohol
    • Severe allergic reaction

    If severe symptoms persist without an identifiable cause, seek professional medical diagnosis at a hospital.
