Diagnosing Diabetes

  • Methods of Diagnosis

    Diagnosing diabetes involves various methods, each typically requiring confirmation on a separate day. Learn about the tests used to identify diabetes and prediabetes.

  • Glycated Hemoglobin (A1C) Test

    The A1C test is a common diagnostic tool for prediabetes and diabetes. This test, done without fasting, measures the percentage of blood sugar attached to hemoglobin over two or three months, providing an average blood sugar level.

    • If A1C is 6.5% or higher: Indicates diabetes
    • If A1C is 5.7 to 6.4%: Indicates prediabetes
    • If A1C is under 5.7%: Indicates normal blood sugar level

  • Fasting Plasma Sugar (FPG) Test

    This test, requiring at least 8 hours of fasting, measures blood sugar levels in the morning. It provides accurate results for diagnosing diabetes or prediabetes.

  • Random Blood Sugar (RPG) Test

    A non-fasting test conducted at any time, the RPG test checks blood sugar levels. Results of 200 mg/dL or higher may indicate diabetes.

  • Oral Sugar Tolerance Test (OGTT)

    For this test, fasting is required, and a sugar-containing liquid is consumed. Blood sugar levels are tested every hour for 2 to 3 hours to determine prediabetes or diabetes.

  • Diagnosis for Pregnant Women

    Pregnant women undergo specific tests, primarily the oral sugar tolerance test and sugar challenge test, between the 24th and 28th weeks of pregnancy.

    • Sugar Challenge Test: Non-fasting test, checks blood sugar one hour after consuming a sugar-containing liquid.
    • Oral Sugar Tolerance Test (OGTT): Follows if the sugar challenge test result is high (over 140 mg/dL), confirming diabetes during pregnancy.

  • Understanding Blood Sugar Ranges

    Knowing the normal ranges of blood sugar is essential for managing diabetes. Blood sugar levels vary before and after meals, and age-specific ranges are observed.

  • Normal Ranges for Adults

    As per diabetes.co.uk and the American Diabetes Association, adults without diabetes exhibit normal blood sugar levels:

    • Before meals or fasting: 4.0 to 5.4 mmol/L (72 to 99 mg/dL)
    • At least 90 minutes after eating: Under 7.8 mmol/L (140 mg/dL)

  • Normal Ranges for Children

    Children's blood sugar levels vary by age:

    • Children under 6: 4.5 to 11.1 mmol/L (80 to 200 mg/dL) each day
    • Children between 6 and 12: Fasting 4.5 to 10 mmol/L (80 to 180 mg/dL), Before meals 5 to 10 mmol/L (90 to 180 mg/dL), One to two hours after meals under 7.8 mmol/L (140 mg/dL)
    • Teens from 13 to 19: Fasting between 3.9 and 8.3 mmol/L (70 to 150 mg/dL), Before meals from 5 to 7.2 mmol/L (90 to 130 mg/dL), One to two hours after meals under 7.8 mmol/L (140 mg/dL)

  • Hypoglycemia Awareness

    Aside from normal and diabetic ranges, understanding hypoglycemia is crucial. Hypoglycemia occurs when blood sugar drops below 3.89 mmol/L (70 mg/dL), with symptoms appearing below 3 mmol/L (55 mg/dL).
