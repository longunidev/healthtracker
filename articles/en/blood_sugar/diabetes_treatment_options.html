Exploring Diabetes Treatment

  • Treatment of Diabetes

    When diabetes develops, various medications are available for treatment. The type of drugs prescribed depends on the specific type of diabetes an individual has. This article provides insights into the treatment options for both type 1 and type 2 diabetes.

  • Medications For Type 1 Diabetes: Insulin

    Insulin is the most common therapy for treating type 1 diabetes. It works by replacing the insulin that the body cannot produce. Diabetics need to inject insulin under the skin to enter the bloodstream, as oral intake is not feasible due to breakdown during digestion.

    Different types of insulin include:

    • Rapid-acting insulin: Starts working in about 15 minutes, peaks after an hour, and lasts for 2 to 4 hours.
    • Short-acting insulin: Works within 30 minutes, peaks in 2 to 3 hours, and lasts for 3 to 6 hours.
    • Intermediate-acting insulin: Starts working 2 to 4 hours after injection, peaks 4 to 12 hours after injection, with effects lasting 12 to 18 hours.
    • Long-acting insulin: Reaches the bloodstream several hours after injection, providing a continuous, non-peaking effect for 24 hours or longer.

    Insulin may also be prescribed for gestational diabetes and used in combination with other oral drugs for type 2 diabetes.

  • Oral Medicines For Type 2 Diabetes

    Metformin: Belonging to the biguanide class, Metformin is a commonly used first-line treatment for type 2 diabetes. It reduces liver-produced sugar and enhances insulin utilization. Diarrhea may occur as a side effect.

    Sulphonylureas: Includes glipizide, glimepiride, and glyburide, stimulating insulin release from the pancreas. It can be used alone or in combination with Metformin.

    Other medicines:

    • Gliptins: Accelerate insulin absorption into the bloodstream.
    • Glitazones: Facilitate the body's insulin utilization.
    • Acarbose: Prevents the conversion of ingested carbohydrates into sugar.
    • GLP-1 analogues (exenatide): Accelerate insulin absorption into the bloodstream.
    • SGLT2 inhibitors: Used in combination with other drugs.

    Understanding the medications prescribed can provide valuable insights into managing your condition.
